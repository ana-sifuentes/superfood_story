What is a superfood?

“Superfood” is not a scientific term but a marketing one.

The term was first used in 1917 by The United Fruit Company, now ‘Chiquita Banana’. In a series of pamphlets, bananas were called superfoods in order to promote sales. Researchers began publishing articles about bananas using the term and in turn, the media printed headlines talking about the ‘super nutritious’ bananas. All of this increased banana sales for years to come.

This particular story will set the script for future superfoods. Superfoods equal money: the global industry is estimated to be worth 137 billion USD, and in 2015 there was a 36% increase of foods claiming the 'superfood' status.

Are 'superfoods' really

Super?

No one ‘superfood’ can cover all nutritional needs.
Marketers use the term ‘superfood’ to stretch the nutritional value of a food, using carefully worded statements that imply extraordinary health benefits.

For example, in 2017 a Canadian news agency, CBC News, found a granola-quinoa product that claimed it lowered cholesterol, controlled blood sugar and prevented cancer. Only after the news agency filed a complaint against them, the company was forced to change their marketing.

This example shows how lack of regulation allows companies to exaggerate the health benefits of a product, putting a lot of weight on the consumer’s decisions. To protect consumers from these predatory practices, the European Union banned the term in 2007, unless it was accompanied by a specific authorised health claim.

However, other areas of the world lack regulation.

Origin of chia

Chia is actually from mesoamerica, present day Mexico and Guatemala.
It was used by various indigenous groups, like the Aztecs -
famous for their strong military, amazing architecture and progressive education.
NEXT

Lost for almost 500 years

Because of the key role chia had in Aztec society and religion, it was banned during colonization and lost for almost 500 years.

Thankfully, chia managed to survive in small, isolated communities.
Chia resurfaced around the 1930s thanks to a strange combination of terracota decorations, nutritional research and the media.

Growing chia

Chia is a very sustainable crop
It grows well in arid regions with low quality soils
and requires little irrigation once established.
You can also grow it on paper towels at home!
NEXT

Chia seed production is very sustainable with a low water footprint and there is no significant damage to soil.
However, look for fair trade chia since farming is a dangerous industry to laborers and they may be exposed to unsafe conditions and abuse.

The Aztecs used a highly efficient agricultural technique called chinampas,
which translates to a square made of canes.
Chinampas were ‘floating’ gardens that maximized water and the soil’s nutrients around them.

Chia is very easy to grow at home, it doesn’t even need soil!
You can soak the seeds for a day, place them on a paper towel and see them grow over the next 2 weeks.
Once they are ready for harvest, you can add them to salads or other dishes.

Chia worldwide

Although the crop is native to Mexico and Guatemala, nowadays the top producers are Bolivia, Paraguay and Argentina.
In 2020 the top importers were the United States, Germany and Spain.

Agriculture sustainability and efficency varies a lot by country, according to the Sustainable Development Goals only 2 countries have achieved this.

What will happen with chia in the future?
Most likely chia production will increase in the next couple of years,
but let's explore 3 potential scenarios.
NEXT

When the global demand for chia started to increase in the early 2010s, overproduction followed creating an inflation period, however the market stabilized around 2016. If the trend continues, more and more people will want to consume chia and stable production would follow.

It might seem unlikely, but history can repeat itself. The trend of superfoods seems to be in its heyday, with a market that grows every year. However, a new trend could come where superfoods are completely eradicated. Chia faced eradication once and since trends come and go, chia could fall again into obscurity.

Chia is also the name of a new type of cryptocurrency that claims to be more eco-friendly than its counterparts since the technology behind it uses less power. This could mean chia will be a sustainable way to handle crypto transactions. However, the chia coin launch has disrupted the short term supply of disk drives. Cryptocurrencies change day by day, so the story keeps developing.

What now?

Food is something so essential that we often overlook how complex it is.
Superfoods used to have a role in indigenous diets, colonization and globalization have obscured this fact.
While some of these crops are more nutritionally dense end even more sustainable, marketers have stretched their benefits to increase sales.
Consumers are vulnerable to misinformation but their purchases end up shaping agriculture practices around the world.
One small seed can have a giant impact.

Maybe you are wondering if you should you eat chia?
The answer isn't that simple, there are lots of things to consider,
sometimes the best solution is asking more questions.
