THE HISTORY OF SUPERFOODS

Superfoods are considered more nutritionally dense than other foods.
However, this marketing term can be misleading, leaving
consumers vulnerable and the foods themselves a mystery.

But first…

What is a superfood?

“Superfood” is a marketing term not a scientific one.

The term was first used in 1917 by The United Fruit Company, now ‘Chiquita Banana’ in a series of pamphlets to promote sales.
Researchers published articles using the term and in turn, the media printed headlines talking about the ‘super nutritious’ bananas.
All of this increased banana sales for years to come.

This particular story will set the script for future superfoods.

Superfoods equal money:
• Between 2011 and 2015 there was a 202% increase in the number of new products that claimed the 'super'status.
• In 2018 the global industry was estimated to be worth 137 billion USD.
• And according to Google Ngrams, the use of the term has increased since 2014.

Are 'superfoods' really

Super?

Not really.
No one ‘superfood’ can cover all nutritional needs.

Marketers use the term ‘superfood’ to stretch the nutritional value of a food, using carefully worded statements that imply extraordinary health benefits.

For example, in 2017 a Canadian news agency, CBC News, found a granola-quinoa product that claimed it lowered cholesterol, controlled blood sugar and prevented cancer.

Only after the news agency filed a complaint against them, the company was forced to change their marketing.

This example shows how lack of regulation allows companies to exaggerate the health benefits of a product, leaving consumers vulnerable and putting a lot of weight on their decisions.

To protect consumers from these predatory practices, the European Union banned the term in 2007, unless it was accompanied by a specific authorised health claim.

However, most of the world lacks regulation.

The complexity of food systems

With superfoods we have a glimpse on how complex food systems are. There are interconnected systems in play that affect what food is available to us and there are many steps before we can even select it.

The plot thickens...

Indigenous foods

The story is even more complicated when it comes to foods with an indigenous origin.
These foods used to play a key role in ancient civilizations but colonization
often led to three potential scenarios: exploitation, neglect or eradication.

Exploitation: Tomatoes

Taken from mesoamerica and incorporated into western diets, nowadays they are associated with the Mediterranean cuisine and its cultures.

Neglect: Avocados

Another mesoamerican crop was exported outside the region but it didn't gain popularity until the 21st century.

Eradication: Chia seeds

Due to their high value religious value for the Aztecs, they were prohibited and lost for almost 400 years. Since their ‘rediscovery’ they has become a very popular food.

Origin of chia

Chia is actually from mesoamerica, present day Mexico and Guatemala.
It was used by various indigenous groups, like the Aztecs -
famous for their strong military, amazing architecture and progressive education.
NEXT

Lost for almost 400 years

Because of the key role chia had in Aztec society and religion, it was banned during colonization and lost for almost 400 years.

Thankfully, chia managed to survive in small, isolated communities.
Chia resurfaced around the 1930s thanks to a strange combination of terracota decorations, nutritional research and ,the media.

Chia sales increased around 2009,when the >EU recognized it as a novel food, authorizing it in the region.

Growing chia

Chia is a very sustainable crop.
It grows well in arid regions even with low quality soils
and requires little irrigation once established.
And you can also grow it on paper towels at home!
NEXT

Modern Farming

Chia seed production is very sustainable with a low water footprint and there is no significant damage to soil. It also doesn't need any sprays since the essentials oils in chia are natural insect repellants.
However, look for fair trade chia seeds since agriculture is one of the most dangerous industries. Workers might be exposed to harsh and unsafe working conditions, low wages and other forms of abuse.

Chinampas

The Aztecs used a highly efficient agricultural technique called chinampas,
which translates to a square made of canes.
Chinampas were ‘floating’ gardens that maximized resources around them. By absorbing nutrients from the lake bed and being constantly irrigated by the water that sorrounds them, they are highly efficient.

Microgreens

Chia is very easy to grow at home, it doesn’t even need soil!
You can soak the seeds for a day, place them on a paper towel and see them grow over the next 2 weeks.
Once they are ready for harvest,when they are about 3cm in height, you can add them to salads or other dishes.

Chia worldwide

Although the crop is native to Mexico and Guatemala, nowadays the top producers are Bolivia, Paraguay and Argentina.

In 2020 the top importers were the United States, Germany and Spain.

Trends continue

When the global demand for chia started to increase in the early 2010s, overproduction followed creating an inflation period, however the market stabilized around 2016. If the trend continues, more and more people will want to consume chia and stable production would follow.

The global chia market for 2021 was evaluated in 66.5 million USD and it projected to keep growing at around 5.8% annually.

Eradication

It might seem unlikely, but history can repeat itself. The trend of superfoods seems to be in its heyday, with a market that grows every year. However, a new trend could come where superfoods are completely eradicated. Chia faced eradication once and since trends come and go, chia could fall again into obscurity.

Cryptochia

Chia is also the name of a new type of cryptocurrency that claims to be more eco-friendly than its counterparts since the technology behind it uses less power. This could mean chia will be a sustainable way to handle crypto transactions.
However, the chia coin launch has disrupted the short term supply of disk drives.
Cryptocurrencies and the market around them, change day by day, so the story keeps developing.

What now?

Food is something so essential that we often overlook how complex it is.

Superfoods used to have a role in indigenous diets, colonization and globalization have obscured this fact.

While some of these crops are more nutritionally dense end even more sustainable, marketers have stretched their benefits to increase sales.

Consumers are vulnerable to misinformation but their purchases end up shaping agriculture practices around the world.

Maybe you are wondering if you should you eat chia?
The answer isn't that simple, there are lots of things to consider,
sometimes the best solution is asking more questions.

One small seed can have a giant impact.
